L20: Media Matters: Ethics for a Responsible Tomorrow (G.S. Chauhan)

From PDF: "Media in the Mirror: Ethics in the Age of Truth and Technology"

⭐ Media Ethics: System of principles guiding action based on personal, professional, social and moral values in information communication.

Core Principles: Truth, Accuracy, Objectivity, Respect for dignity, Privacy.

Major Issues

  • Fake News: Misinformation (false, no intent to harm), Disinformation (false, deliberate harm), Malinformation (real info used to harm, e.g., old photo).
  • Sensationalism: “yellow journalism”, shock over facts.
  • Stereotyping: simplified representation; quantity (under/over) and quality (positive/negative).
  • Deepfakes: AI‑generated fake images/videos; challenges trust, consent.
  • Algorithmic Bias: selection, confirmation, representation, amplification biases.
  • Emerging: Influencer transparency, Media ownership concentration, Agenda setting, TRP‑driven content, Digital surveillance.

Ethical Frameworks & Regulation (India)

Press Council of India: objectivity, no defamation. ASCI: truthful advertising. Digital: DPDP Act 2023, IT Rules 2021 (three‑tier grievance). Fact‑checking bodies. Media literacy essential.

L21: Ethics in Context: Across Time, Place, and Society (Devika)

From PDF: "Ethics in context"

⭐ Ethics: System of moral principles guiding human conduct. Not absolute—constructed, shaped by historical contexts, social structures, power dynamics, culture.

Ethics across time

  • Ancient: virtue, duty, harmony — Aristotle, Confucius, Buddha, Kautilya.
  • Medieval: religion‑dominated — Augustine, Aquinas; Adi Shankaracharya, Ramanujacharya (dharma, bhakti).
  • Modern: reason, rights, consequences — Kant (duty), Mill (utilitarianism); Gandhi, Ambedkar (non‑violence, social justice).

Cultural relativism vs Ethical absolutism: CR – values understood within culture; EA – universal moral principles. Ethics operate in‑between.

Ethics and society: shaped by family, religion, law; moral expectations vary by class, gender, caste. Tool of social control and reform.

Ethics, power, ideology: shaped by those in power; colonial ethics vs indigenous. Not neutral.

Everyday life ethics: dilemmas, care, responsibility, empathy; professional ethics (doctor‑patient). Ethics is lived practice.

Global ethics: oriental‑occidental traditions; glocal balance. Modern challenges: environment, Gen AI, bio‑medicine.

Literary ethics: narratives build ethical imagination; ethics of representation.

Ethics as living practice: dynamic, contextual, dialogic; requires critical thinking, empathy, questioning norms.

L23: Approaches to Ethics and Their Applications in Human Life (K.N. Sachdev)

From PDF: "Approaches to Ethics"

⭐ Ethics: Study of values to live a good human life. Two questions: How should I live? (character/virtues) What should I do? (principles/rules).

Aristotle – Virtue Ethics

Teleological: goal is eudaimonia (happiness/good life). Human function is to exercise rationality in accordance with virtue. Doctrine of Mean: virtue lies between deficiency and excess (courage = mean between cowardice and foolhardiness). Practice forms virtuous habits.

John Stuart Mill – Utilitarian Ethics

Actions right if they promote happiness, wrong if reverse. Greatest Happiness Principle: maximize overall happiness. Higher pleasures (intellect) vs lower (body). Motive: social feelings of humankind.

Immanuel Kant – Deontological Ethics

Rightness depends on duty, not consequences. Categorical Imperative: 1) “Act only according to that maxim by which you can will it become universal law” (e.g., false promise). 2) “Treat humanity always as an end, never merely as a means” (e.g., older employees). Moral worth from acting out of respect for moral law.

Applied Ethics & Professional Ethics

Whistle‑blowing: voluntary release of nonpublic info as moral protest. Conditions: moral importance, facts, internal channels exhausted, appropriate audience, role responsibility, chance of success. Loyalty = commitment to true interests of organization, which must align with public interest.

Case: Mr. Reddy (engineer) followed rules over supervisor — Kant’s respect for moral law.
