L15: Society and Social Movement (Madhurima Das)

From PDF: "Social Movements"

⭐ Social Movement: “A form of collective behaviour in which a large number of people are united in an attempt to promote or resist social change.” (Mauss)

Key features: large-scale collective action, shared dissatisfaction, goal to promote/resist change, informal & formal participation.

Types (6 categories)

  • Alternative: limited change in specific individuals (e.g., anti‑drinking campaign).
  • Redemptive: total change in individuals (e.g., religious conversion).
  • Reformative: limited change in entire society (e.g., women’s suffrage).
  • Transformative: total change in society (e.g., revolution).
  • Reactionary: return to past values (e.g., fundamentalist movements).
  • Expressive: personal feelings, alternative lifestyles (e.g., hippie movement).
  • Transnational: global orientation (human rights, environment).

Life‑Cycle (Armand Mauss) – 5 stages:

1. Incipience (discontent, leaders emerge) → 2. Coalescence (groups form, manifestos, resources) → 3. Institutionalization (formal org, risk of co‑optation) → 4. Fragmentation (conflicts, rebel factions) → 5. Demise (goals accomplished or movement ends, legacy).

Feminist Movement – Three Waves

First (1830s‑1920s): suffrage, legal equality. Second (1960s‑80s): “personal is political”, de Beauvoir. Third (1990s‑): intersectionality, gender binaries challenged.

Environmental Movements

  • Chipko: women hugged trees (1730 Amrita Devi, later Uttarakhand).
  • Narmada Bachao Andolan: Medha Patkar, Baba Amte against large dams.
  • Nuclear testing protests: Pokhran, New Mexico — indigenous rights.
  • Ecofeminism: domination of nature linked to exploitation of women (patriarchy).

Social foundations: shared grievance + collective identity + resource mobilization sustain movements.

L16: State-Society Relationship and Political Obligation (Veena Ramachandran)

From PDF: "State, Society and Political Obligation"

⭐ Political Obligation: Moral or legal duty of citizens to obey the laws of the state. Key questions: Why obey? When is disobedience justified?

Classical Foundations

  • Socrates (Plato’s Crito): tacit consent by living in the state; obey even unjust sentence to uphold law.
  • Plato: philosopher kings. Aristotle: humans are political animals; obligation from belonging to polis.
  • Divine Right Theory: kings rule by God’s authority (Aquinas, Bossuet).

Social Contract Tradition

  • Hobbes (Leviathan): state of nature “solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, short”; surrender rights for security.
  • Locke: natural rights (life, liberty, property); government must protect; right to revolt.
  • Rousseau (Social Contract): “Man is born free, but everywhere in chains”; General Will; obey laws we collectively make.

“No taxation without representation” — American Revolution, James Otis; democratic legitimacy.

Transcendentalism (19th c.): Emerson (self‑reliance), Thoreau — civil disobedience against unjust laws (refused tax over slavery). Influenced Gandhi, MLK.

Civil Disobedience: nonviolent, public, conscientious law‑breaking (Rawls, Arendt).

Legitimacy (Max Weber): Traditional, Charismatic, Legal‑rational authority.

State vs Society: state = political org, laws, territory, sovereignty; society = social org, norms, precedes state.

Non‑Western: Confucian harmony, hierarchy, duty; colonial legacy reshaped state‑society.

L17: Power Dynamics and Socialization (Tanu Shukla)

From PDF: "State Structures, Societal Organisations, Psychological factors and Social dynamics"

Society experienced through group memberships (caste, gender). Mind categorizes “us vs them” → identity, bias, stereotypes.

⭐ Group Polarization: Discussion leads to more extreme opinions; reinforcement, conformity. Prejudice: preconceived negative attitude (cognitive stereotype, affective emotion, behavioural discrimination).

Reducing bias: Common Ingroup Identity (“We are all Indians”), Cross‑categorization (overlapping identities), Superordinate goals (disaster relief cooperation).

Attribution & Biases

  • Fundamental Attribution Error: overestimate internal causes for others’ behaviour.
  • Actor‑Observer Effect: own behaviour attributed to situation, others’ to disposition.
  • Self‑Serving Bias: success internal, failure external.

Conformity, Compliance, Obedience

Normative Social Influence: desire to be liked. Informational: desire to be right. Compliance techniques: Foot‑in‑the‑door, Door‑in‑the‑face, Ingratiation, Playing hard to get.

⭐ Milgram’s Experiment (1963): 65% obeyed fully, administering max shocks. Amplifiers: legitimacy, proximity, gradual escalation. Shows ordinary people can commit harmful acts under authority.

State mechanisms of compliance: chain of command diffuses responsibility, bureaucratization, symbols of authority, institutional legitimacy.

Groupthink: faulty decision‑making, suppression of dissent, illusion of unanimity. Realistic appraisal: encourage critical evaluation, diverse perspectives.

Acculturation: cultural/psychological change from prolonged intergroup contact.

L18: Digital Ecosystem and Society (Virendra Singh Nirban)

From PDF: "Technology, Society & Governance in India"

4 As of ICT: Availability, Accessibility, Affordability, Adoptability. UNESCO SDG Target 9.C—universal internet access.

Access Unpacked (Tony Roberts, 5 A's): Availability, Affordability, Awareness, Accessibility, Ability.

Universal Service Obligation Fund (USOF) India: bridges rural‑urban digital divide, addresses market failure.

⭐ India’s DPI (Digital Public Infrastructure) Model: Identity (Aadhaar), Payments (UPI), Consent (DEPA). Public rails, private innovation. Treats foundational systems as public goods.

India Stack Components

  • Aadhaar: 1.38B enrolled, 70M+ auth/month.
  • UPI: 13.4B transactions (Mar 2024), 350+ banks. Near‑zero cost.
  • DigiLocker: 350M users, legal document storage.
  • ABHA/CoWIN: health IDs, 2.2B vaccine doses managed.

Technology reshapes society and vice versa. Challenges: surveillance creep, data breaches (ICMR 815M records), function creep, biometric exclusion (8.1Cr failed auths). Privacy as fundamental right (Puttaswamy 2017).

Regulatory landscape: DPDP Act 2023, IT Rules 2021, RBI frameworks, Telecom Act 2023. Gaps: AI, algorithmic accountability.

Key tensions: inclusion vs privacy, innovation vs accountability, state power vs rights, mandatory scale vs meaningful consent.

L19: Governance System in India (Sanjiv Kumar Choudhary)

From PDF: "Governance System in India"

⭐ Separation of Powers (Montesquieu, The Spirit of Laws): Legislature (makes law), Executive (implements), Judiciary (interprets). To prevent tyranny, protect liberty, ensure accountability. “Power divided is freedom preserved.”

Legislature (Parliament)

President (part of Parliament), Rajya Sabha (Council of States, permanent), Lok Sabha (House of People, every 5 yrs). Functions: law‑making, financial control (budget), executive check (no‑confidence motion), constitutional amendment.

Executive

Political (PM + Council of Ministers) + Permanent (Civil Services: IAS, IPS). Real executive power with PM. Functions: policy, administration, diplomacy, emergency powers.

Judiciary

Supreme Court → High Courts → District Courts. Judicial Review: strike down unconstitutional laws/actions. Guardian of Constitution, protector of Fundamental Rights (writ jurisdiction).

Checks and Balances

Legislature over Executive (no‑confidence, budget), Executive over Legislature (summon/dissolve, assent), Judiciary over both (judicial review, contempt), Legislature & Executive over Judiciary (impeachment, amendment respecting basic structure).

Challenges: fusion of Executive‑Legislature, political pressure, case pendency.
