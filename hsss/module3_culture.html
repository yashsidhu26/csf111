L10: Understanding Ideology (Paul Mathew)

From PDF: "What is Ideology?"

⭐ Ideology: A system of ideas that shapes how we understand reality and justifies power relations. Not neutral — it can maintain or challenge power.

Key Thinkers & Concepts

  • Antoine Destutt de Tracy (1796): Coined “science of ideas”.
  • Karl Marx (1845): Ideology as “false consciousness” — the ruling class's ideas dominate. Base (economy) shapes superstructure (culture, law, ideology).
  • Antonio Gramsci: Cultural Hegemony — the bourgeoisie rules by making its values seem ‘common sense’, securing consent.
  • Frankfurt School (Adorno & Horkheimer): Culture Industry — mass‑produced culture creates passive consumers and prevents critical thought.
  • Louis Althusser: Ideological State Apparatuses (ISAs) like family, school, church — “interpellate” individuals into subjects. Repressive State Apparatus (RSA) uses force.
  • Michel Foucault: Power/Knowledge — truth is produced by systems (medicine, law) to dominate. Power is productive, not just repressive.
  • Slavoj Žižek: “They know very well, but they are doing it anyway” — ideology is not false consciousness but a cynical disavowal.
Three critical questions to analyze ideology:
1. Whose material interests are served?
2. Who owns/controls the means of production?
3. Who owns/controls the medium of communication?

Example: AI coffee shop tracking barista productivity — ideology of efficiency surveillance.

Science & Ideology: Eugenics, Social Darwinism, Lysenkoism, Cold War funding of physics, Green Revolution ignoring social consequences.

L11: Culture and Communication (Pushp Lata)

From PDF: "Understanding Self and Society through Culture"

⭐ Culture: “Collective programming of the mind” (Hofstede). Complex whole: knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom (Tylor). Shared beliefs, values, practices transmitted socially (M.N. Srinivas).

Iceberg Model (Edward T. Hall)

Visible: food, dress, language. Invisible: values, beliefs, norms, expectations. Most cultural influences are hidden but powerful.

Indian collectivism: person defined through family/roles (T.N. Madan). Joint families, group loyalty, family approval still important.

⭐ Hofstede’s Cultural Dimensions (India):
• Power Distance: high (77/100) – hierarchy accepted.
• Individualism vs Collectivism: collectivist.
• Uncertainty Avoidance: medium.
• Long‑Term Orientation: high.
• Indulgence vs Restraint: restraint.

High‑context (India) vs Low‑context (West) communication: implicit meaning, indirect refusal “We’ll try” vs. explicit “No”.

Polychronic time (India): people > schedules, multitasking, functions run late. Monochronic (West): clock‑bound, linear.

Values → Traditions → Norms: Deep beliefs (respect for elders) → customs (Diwali, Namaste) → rules (remove shoes).

L12: Reading Images (Shriya Raina)

From PDF: "Reading Images"

⭐ Visual Culture: Production, consumption, distribution of images and visual content shared and understood by communities. Images are constructed, not neutral.

Reading images: differentiate What is shown, What is suggested, What meaning is generated.

Examples from lecture:

  • 3‑panel comic classroom: different POVs, details emphasized (cell phone, back benchers) — image constructs meaning.
  • Pepsi AI Christmas ad: backlash — “dad got fired because AI replaced him”. Context: AI replacing animation jobs.
  • Stock photos criminal man/woman: men with weapons, violent; women repentant, no weapons — gendering of crime.
  • Shutterstock “scientific research”: messy, private, secretive — visuals construct “knowledge”.
  • ChatGPT image “poor person begging”: fills gaps with stereotypes — urban sidewalk, western look, ignores country/age. Diffusion model reproduces dominant visual tropes.
  • Kevin Carter’s Sudan photograph (1993): African poverty as tragedy, need of white saviour, ignores colonial exploitation.
  • Dharavi slum tourism: aestheticizing poverty, “heart of Mumbai” narrative, tour packages.
Key findings: Transparency of images is a myth; images both reflect and construct reality. Visual ≠ Visible. Reading requires visual literacy — shared codes. Constant repetition normalizes certain representations (e.g., India = snake charmers).

L13: How Societies Tell Stories: Theatre, Culture and Social Life (Prateek)

From PDF: "How Societies Tell Stories"

Prehistoric theatre: Mesolithic cave paintings (Lakhajoor, MP) — masks, dance, shamans. Characteristics: imitation of animals, ritualistic structure, sequestered performance space.

⭐ Ritual (Victor Turner): “A stereotyped sequence of activities involving gestures, words, objects, performed in a sequestered place, designed to influence preternatural entities or forces.”

Sanskrit theatre – Natyasastra (Bharata Muni): Fifth Veda created by Brahma to overcome lust, greed, jealousy of Treta Yuga. Took lyrics from Rigveda, music from Samaveda, gesture from Yajurveda, rasa from Atharvaveda.

First performance: Devas vs Asuras — Asuras disrupted it; Brahma explained theatre is “mimicry of the world”. Subsequent: Amrita Manthana, Tripuradaha.

Theatre as moral instruction and social transformation. Theatre brings together ritual, religion, representation, aesthetic experience.
