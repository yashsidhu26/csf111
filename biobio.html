🆘 The Open-Book Solver

Search for keywords from your exam question to instantly find the concept and exact page number.

Question has a Graph showing DNA Amount changing (1x to 2x) Pg 125, 204
Concept: Cell Cycle & Flow Cytometry.
Action: If DNA doubles, it is passing through the S-Phase. A peak at 1x is G1, a peak at 2x is G2/M phase. If the question mentions mitochondrial DNA vs Nuclear DNA, remember mtDNA replicates independently of the cell cycle!
Question asks to map Restriction Enzymes from an Agarose Gel Pg 220, 226
Concept: Restriction Mapping & Gel Electrophoresis.
Action: Look at the bands. Remember: Smallest fragments travel furthest (bottom of gel). If a circular plasmid is cut once, you get 1 band. If cut twice, you get 2 bands. The sum of the fragments must equal the total plasmid size.
Question involves a specific Toxin/Chemical stopping a bodily function Pg 82, 368, 299
Concept: Enzyme Inhibition or Receptor Blocking.
Action: If it's a nerve gas/toxin (Pg 82), it's likely an enzyme inhibitor (competitive or non-competitive). If it affects heart/breathing, it might block neurotransmitter reuptake (Pg 368) or bind to hemoglobin preventing Oxygen transport (like Carbon Monoxide, Pg 299).
Question asks about calculating ATP yield with a mutant/missing enzyme Pg 95-100, Appendix A-1
Concept: Cellular Respiration Pathways.
Action: Go to the diagrams. Normal Glycolysis = 2 ATP + 2 NADH. Normal TCA = 2 ATP, 6 NADH, 2 FADH2. If the mutant skips a step (like the 2024 PYQ), subtract the specific ATP/NADH generated at that specific step using Appendix A-1/A-2 diagrams.
Question involves Pedigrees, Carriers, and Probability Pg 153-154, 163-164
Concept: Mendelian or Sex-Linked Inheritance.
Action: Is it mostly males affected? It's Sex-Linked Recessive (Pg 163). Do unaffected parents have an affected child? It's Autosomal Recessive (Pg 153). Remember to use the rule of multiplication for probabilities (Pg 152).
Question tracks a food item through the body Pg 264-272
Concept: Digestive System & Macromolecule breakdown.
Action: Carbs start digesting in the mouth (salivary amylase, pg 267). Proteins in the stomach (pepsin/acid, pg 268). Fats in the small intestine (bile/lipase, pg 269). Absorption happens in the small intestine villi (pg 270).
Question compares two cells in the body having different structures/functions Pg 198-200
Concept: Gene Regulation / Differentiation.
Action: ALL somatic cells have the EXACT same DNA. The difference is solely due to Gene Regulation (which genes are turned "on" or "off" via transcription factors, DNA packing, or enhancers).
Question mentions Plasmolysis, flaccid, or turgid cells Pg 84-85
Concept: Osmosis and Water Balance.
Action: Water moves from Hypotonic (low solute) to Hypertonic (high solute). Plant cells want to be hypotonic (turgid). Animal cells will burst in hypotonic environments and shrivel in hypertonic environments.

✍️ BITS Answer Writing Strategy

How to write open-book answers that get full marks. Rule #1: "No justification, NO marks. Irrelevantly long answers are NOT entertained."

2024 PYQ (F1): One of your female colleagues used a hair straightening iron to straighten her curly hair. Based on your learning from the course explain the biochemical concept behind this. [2 Marks]

❌ The "Rambling/Basic" Answer (0.5/2 Marks)

The hair straightener uses heat to make the hair straight. Hair is made of proteins, and the high temperature melts the hair into a straight line. When it cools down, it stays straight because she used the iron to physically pull it.

✅ The "Biochemical" Answer (2/2 Marks)

Hair is composed of the protein keratin. The heat from the iron causes denaturation, breaking the weak hydrogen bonds and disulfide bridges that maintain the protein's secondary and tertiary structures (the curls). Upon cooling in a straight position, new bonds reform, holding the straight structure.

Strategy Tip: Always use the exact biochemical vocabulary from the book (Denaturation, Hydrogen bonds, Tertiary structure). Never use layman terms like "melts".
2023 PYQ (Q1): Indian Government banned polythene bags. Why hasn't a ban been implemented on paper bags, as a cow was seen eating paper bags in the street? Give a suitable biochemical justification. [4 Marks]

❌ The "General Knowledge" Answer (1/4 Marks)

Paper bags are eco-friendly and biodegradable, while plastic bags pollute the environment and choke animals. Cows shouldn't eat either, but paper digests easier than plastic.

✅ The "Course Concept" Answer (4/4 Marks)

Paper bags are made of cellulose, a plant polysaccharide. While cows themselves lack cellulase, their digestive tracts harbor symbiotic bacteria that produce enzymes capable of hydrolyzing cellulose into usable glucose monomers. Polythene is a synthetic polymer that neither the cow nor its microbiome possesses enzymes to break down, leading to deadly intestinal blockage.

Strategy Tip: Connect real-world scenarios to specific book concepts. Mention the molecule (Cellulose), the organism trait (Symbiotic bacteria), and the chemical reaction (Hydrolysis).
2024 PYQ (F4): Aerobic bacteria do not have mitochondria yet they generate ATP. Name the process(es) involved and the site for the same. [2 Marks]

❌ The "Over-explained" Answer (1/2 Marks)

Because they are prokaryotes, they don't have membrane bound organelles like mitochondria. So they just do glycolysis in the cytoplasm which makes 2 ATP, and they don't need the rest of the cycle because they are small.

✅ The "Direct & Accurate" Answer (2/2 Marks)

Processes: Glycolysis, Krebs Cycle, and Electron Transport Chain (ETC).
Sites: Glycolysis and Krebs cycle occur in the cytosol/cytoplasm. The Electron Transport Chain and ATP Synthase are embedded in the plasma membrane.

Strategy Tip: If a question asks to "Name" or "Identify", use bullet points. Do not write paragraphs. BITS professors grade based on a strict keyword rubric.
